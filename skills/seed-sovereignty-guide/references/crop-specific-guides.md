## Brassica Family

### Cabbage

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum (all brassicas cross readily)
**Minimum Population:** 20-50 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial (2 years)

**Year 1:**
1. Plant in spring for fall harvest
2. Harvest heads for eating
3. Select best plants for seed saving (10-20)
4. Leave roots in ground or dig and store

**Overwintering Options:**
- **In ground:** Mulch heavily, protect with straw
- **Root cellar:** Dig roots, store in moist sand at 32-40°F
- **Unheated garage:** Store roots in containers with sand

**Year 2:**
1. Replant roots early spring (6-8 weeks before last frost)
2. Allow plants to bolt and flower
3. Seed pods will form along stem
4. Allow pods to dry and turn brown
5. Harvest when pods rattle (shatter risk)
6. Thresh pods, winnow, dry seeds
7. Store in cool, dry conditions

**Processing:**
- Hand thresh (crush pods)
- Winnow to remove chaff
- Dry 7-14 days
- Seeds are small and round, brown/black

**Viability:** 4 years
**Seeds per pod:** 10-20
**Seeds per plant:** 5,000-10,000

### Broccoli

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 20-50 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial

**Year 1:**
1. Plant spring crop
2. Harvest main head for eating
3. Allow side shoots to go to seed
4. Select best side shoots for seed saving

**Year 2 (if not allowed to go to seed in Year 1):**
1. Overwinter plants (similar to cabbage)
2. Replant in spring
3. Allow to flower and set seed

**Harvest Timing:**
- Harvest seed pods when dry and brown
- Watch for shattering (pods open easily)
- Harvest before full shattering occurs

**Processing:**
- Strip pods from stalks
- Thresh by crushing or shaking
- Winnow carefully (seeds are small)
- Dry thoroughly

**Viability:** 4 years
**Yield:** High - broccoli produces abundant seeds

### Kale

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 20-50 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial

**Year 1:**
1. Plant kale for greens
2. Select best plants for seed saving
3. Leave plants in ground through winter

**Overwintering:**
- Kale overwinters well in many climates
- Mulch base of plants
- Some varieties survive -20°F with protection

**Year 2:**
1. Plants will bolt in spring
2. Yellow flowers form on tall stalks
3. Seed pods develop after flowers fade
4. Allow pods to dry completely
5. Harvest before pods shatter

**Processing:**
- Cut seed stalks
- Place in paper bags to dry further
- Thresh by crushing dry pods
- Winnow to remove chaff

**Viability:** 4 years
**Note:** Kale is very easy to save seeds from - produces abundant seed

## Cucurbit Family

### Squash (Summer and Winter)

**Isolation:** 1/2 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 6-12 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Crossing Rules:**
- C. pepo crosses with: zucchini, acorn squash, pumpkins (some), gourds
- C. moschata crosses with: butternut squash, some pumpkins
- C. maxima crosses with: hubbard, buttercup, turban squash
- Different species DO NOT cross

**Selection:**
1. Mark fruits on best plants early in season
2. Allow marked fruits to fully mature (past eating stage)
3. Fruits should be hard and skin difficult to pierce

**Harvest Timing:**
- Summer squash: Allow to grow very large, skin hardens
- Winter squash: Harvest when stem shrivels, leave in storage 1-2 months

**Processing:**
1. Cut fruit open lengthwise
2. Scoop seeds into container
3. Add water, let ferment 2-3 days
4. Stir daily
5. Pour off floating debris
6. Rinse seeds thoroughly
7. Spread to dry (7-14 days)
8. Test dryness (seeds should snap)

**Viability:**
- Summer squash: 4 years
- Winter squash: 4-6 years

**Yield:** 100-400 seeds per fruit

### Cucumber

**Isolation:** 1/2 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 12-20 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Selection:**
1. Mark fruits early in season
2. Allow fruit to turn yellow/orange
3. Fruit should be soft and overripe

**Harvest Timing:**
- Harvest when fruit is yellow and soft
- Leave on vine until vine dies back
- Fruit may develop bitter taste (don't eat)

**Processing:**
1. Cut fruit in half lengthwise
2. Scoop seeds into container
3. Ferment 2-3 days
4. Rinse thoroughly
5. Remove membrane from seeds
6. Dry 7-14 days

**Viability:** 5-10 years (excellent longevity)
**Yield:** 100-200 seeds per fruit

### Melons

**Isolation:** 1/2 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 12-20 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Selection:**
1. Mark best fruits early
2. Allow to fully ripen (past eating stage)
3. Fruit may crack or split when very ripe

**Harvest Timing:**
- Cantaloupe: Netting fully developed, stem shrivels
- Watermelon: Ground spot yellow, tendril dry
- Wait until vine dies back

**Processing:**
1. Cut melon open
2. Scoop seeds into container
3. Rinse seeds to remove pulp
4. No fermentation needed (optional)
5. Dry thoroughly (7-14 days)

**Viability:** 5-10 years
**Yield:** 200-600 seeds per fruit

## Nightshade Family

### Peppers

**Isolation:** 500 feet minimum (bagging recommended)
**Minimum Population:** 6-10 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Crossing Note:** Hot peppers and sweet peppers can cross - isolate by type if preserving heat level

**Selection:**
1. Mark fruits early in season
2. Allow to fully ripen (often red/orange/yellow)
3. Fruit should be fully colored and slightly soft

**Harvest Timing:**
- Harvest when fruit is fully ripe
- Some varieties turn from green to red/orange
- Wait until color is uniform

**Processing:**
1. Cut fruit in half
2. Scrape out seeds with spoon
3. Place seeds in water bowl
4. Viable seeds sink, debris floats
5. Pour off floating material
6. Rinse seeds
7. Spread on paper towel
8. Dry 7-10 days

**Viability:**
- Sweet peppers: 2-3 years
- Hot peppers: 3-4 years

**Yield:** 50-200 seeds per fruit

**Drying Note:** Pepper seeds are small and lightweight. Dry in single layer, not stacked.

### Eggplant

**Isolation:** 500 feet minimum
**Minimum Population:** 6-10 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Selection:**
1. Mark fruits early
2. Allow to overripen significantly
3. Fruit will turn dull color, skin wrinkles

**Harvest Timing:**
- Harvest when fruit is past eating stage
- Fruit may be yellow/brown
- Seeds should be fully developed

**Processing:**
1. Cut fruit open
2. Scoop out seeds with spoon
3. Rub to remove pulp and membrane
4. Rinse seeds thoroughly
5. Dry 7-10 days

**Viability:** 4-6 years
**Yield:** 50-200 seeds per fruit

## Legume Family

### Beans

**Isolation:** 10-20 feet (rarely crosses)
**Minimum Population:** 20-40 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Selection:**
1. Mark best plants early in season
2. Select for disease resistance, vigor, yield
3. Avoid diseased or stunted plants

**Harvest Timing:**
- Allow pods to dry completely on vine
- Pods should be crispy and brown
- Beans should rattle when shaken

**Processing:**
1. Harvest dry pods
2. Thresh (stomp or crush in bag)
3. Winnow to remove pods
4. Dry thoroughly (7-14 days)
5. Test dryness (beans should shatter when hit with hammer)

**Viability:** 3-4 years
**Yield:** 3-10 seeds per pod, 20-50 pods per plant

### Peas

**Isolation:** 50 feet (occasional bee pollination)
**Minimum Population:** 20-40 plants
**Life Cycle:** Annual

**Selection:**
1. Mark earliest-producing plants
2. Select for vigor and disease resistance
3. Avoid powdery mildew-prone plants

**Harvest Timing:**
- Allow pods to dry completely
- Pods turn brown and papery
- Peas rattle inside pods

**Processing:**
1. Harvest dry pods
2. Thresh (threshing or crushing)
3. Winnow to remove pods
4. Dry thoroughly

**Viability:** 3-4 years
**Yield:** 4-10 seeds per pod

## Root Vegetables (Biennials)

### Carrots

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum (Queen Anne's Lace is wild carrot)
**Minimum Population:** 32-64 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial (2 years)

**Year 1:**
1. Plant carrots for harvest
2. Select best roots for seed saving
3. Choose for shape, color, taste

**Overwintering:**
- **In ground:** Mulch heavily with straw or leaves (zones 5-8)
- **Root cellar:** Store roots in moist sand at 32-40°F
- **Pit storage:** Bury roots in pit with mulch (cold climates)

**Year 2:**
1. Replant roots in early spring
2. Space 6-12 inches apart
3. Plants will send up seed stalks
4. White umbrella-shaped flowers appear
5. Seeds form in umbels
6. Harvest when umbels turn brown and dry
7. Seeds shatter easily - harvest before full shattering

**Processing:**
1. Cut umbels or entire stalk
2. Place in paper bag to finish drying
3. Rub umbels to release seeds
4. Winnow carefully (seeds are small with bristles)
5. Dry thoroughly

**Viability:** 3-5 years
**Yield:** 1,000-4,000 seeds per plant

**Note:** Carrot seeds have tiny bristles - these can irritate skin. Wear gloves when processing.

### Beets

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 16-32 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial

**Year 1:**
1. Plant beets for harvest
2. Select best roots (size, shape, color)
3. Avoid those bolting prematurely

**Overwintering:**
- **In ground:** Mulch heavily (zones 6-8)
- **Root cellar:** Store in moist sand at 32-40°F
- **Unheated garage:** Store in containers with damp sand

**Year 2:**
1. Replant roots early spring
2. Space 12-18 inches apart
3. Plants send up seed stalks
4. Greenish-white flowers form
5. Seed pods develop (burrs)
6. Harvest when brown and dry

**Processing:**
1. Cut seed stalks
2. Thresh to release seeds from burrs
3. Winnow to remove debris
4. Dry thoroughly

**Viability:** 4-6 years
**Yield:** 500-2,000 seeds per plant

**Note:** Beet seeds are actually fruit clusters containing multiple seeds. One "seed" may produce 2-4 seedlings.

### Onions

**Isolation:** 1 mile minimum
**Minimum Population:** 20-50 plants
**Life Cycle:** Biennial

**Year 1:**
1. Plant onions for harvest
2. Select best bulbs (size, shape, storage quality)
3. Cure bulbs before overwintering

**Overwintering:**
- **Root cellar:** Store at 32-40°F, 60-70% humidity
- **Unheated garage:** In mesh bags with good air circulation
- **In ground:** Mulch heavily (only mild climates)

**Year 2:**
1. Replant bulbs early spring
2. Plant with necks just above soil
3. Plants send up flower stalks
4. White flower umbels form
5. Black seeds develop in umbels
6. Harvest when umbels shatter easily

**Processing:**
1. Cut umbels before full shattering
2. Place in paper bag to finish drying
3. Rub umbels to release black seeds
4. Winnow to remove chaff
5. Dry thoroughly (onion seeds lose viability quickly)

**Viability:** 1-2 years (very short!)
**Yield:** 500-2,000 seeds per plant

**Critical:** Onion seeds must be used quickly. Always save fresh seed annually.
